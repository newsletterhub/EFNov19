I love, love, love urban green spaces, and one of our city's best outdoor havens — 500-acre Hudson River Park — is adding some fantastic new offerings in the coming years as part of an ongoing $1 billion revitalization. Spanning the riverfront for more than 4.5 miles, from Tribeca to Midtown, HRPK already delivers a dizzying array of passive outdoor space and recreation areas, including lawns, plazas, dog parks, skate parks, playgrounds, sports fields and even areas for kayaking, trapeze and mini-golf. 
 
Recently unveiled plans for Pier 97, the northernmost pier in HRPK, detail a $38 million renovation that will add nearly 2 acres of otherworldly outdoor space along the Hell's Kitchen section of the park. Located off of 12th Avenue and 57th Street, Pier 97 will contain a series of interconnected spaces that include playgrounds, sculpture gardens and esplanades envisioned by design firm Melk. There will be foliage and rock formations to block out West Side Highway commotion, plus restrooms and restaurants to serve visitors. The entire purpose of the pier "is to serve as a reprieve from the city with a space that almost feels as though you’re stepping out of it," says Madelyn Wils, the president and CEO of Hudson River Park Trust. Groundbreaking is slated for next fall for a Spring 2022 opening. 
 
Back in July, plans were announced for the opposite end of HRPK, where New Yorkers will soon bask in Manhattan's first public beach. Gansevoort Peninsula, until recently a Department of Sanitation facility, will be the site of a 5.65-acre park and beach also scheduled for a mid-2022 opening. James Corner Field Operations (JCFO), notable for their contributions to the High Line and Domino Park, are in charge of designing the space located from Gansevoort Street to Little West 12th Street, along what was known as 13th Avenue. Here, we'll find a sandy beach with kayaking and seating areas plus a salt marsh, a large sports field and an art installation called Day's End, commissioned by the Whitney Museum. 
 
Parks are integral to life in the city, and Hudson River Park, in particular, serves an important need in the outdoor-space-starved areas along the west side of Manhattan. “If you want people to live in the city, you have to provide these kinds of amenities. That’s the thing that we hear most from families: That if it weren’t for the park, they would move out of the city, because they wouldn’t have a place for their kids to play," says Wils. "It’s a large driver to keep people in Manhattan."
